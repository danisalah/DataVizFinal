Visualizing Crime in San Francisco



In San Francisco, homelessness and crime have become emergent issues for residents. However, most news stories (and intuition from spending time in the city) suggest that crime rates are not evenly dispersed throughout. Simultaneously, San Francisco is well known for its very hilly topography, with some of the ‘nicer’ neighborhoods sitting at the top of the steepest hills. Through this project, we aim to discover the relationship between these elevation features and different types of crime.



Get Started

San Francisco is well known to be a center of technology and innovation, but as the city continually evolves, homelessness and crime have made the city feel less safe for citizens and tourists alike. San Francisco had the highest per-capita property crime rates in 2017, and has an overall crime rate 3.7 times the national average. Understanding the drivers of these rates is challenging, as they are symptoms of larger, systemic problems. However, visualizing and engaging with crime data can allow for trends to be unearthed.

In building these visualizations, we wanted to explore two main questions. Firstly, as visualization allows for trends in the data to come into focus, what can we learn from exploring the city’s crime rates over the last several years? Our second goal is a personal one for one group member. For the last three years, Danielle has lived at the top of one of San Francisco’s famous hills. During daily commute up the hill, she has often pondered if neighborhoods at higher elevations experience reduced crime rates, simply because perpetrators are reluctant to brave the climbs.

The below visualizations allow users to explore these and other questions about crime rates in San Francisco. They do not intend to provide concrete answers about drivers of crime but rather to highlight trends that are visible within the data. They are built using Police Department Incident Reports from San Francisco’s open data portal, which can be accessed here.

Overview of all crimes recorded in San Francisco between 2013 and 2017


Before any true insights can be gained, it is important to first be oriented to the data. These views answer high level questions. As you engage with the dashboard, consider the following:

  • How do crime rates differ by neighborhood?
  • How have crime rates changed over time?
  • How does crime frequency change throughout the day?

Using the table (first quadrant) to filter the dashboard allows for deeper investigation into a single year, a single crime type, or a combination of each.

Investigating different types of crime by neighborhood


After some high level exploration, some initial conclusions may be drawn about which neighborhoods experience disproportionate crime. These views intend to explore that further. The heatmap immediately draws the eye to more common crime types, across the city as well as in specific neighborhoods. Using the table (top) to filter the dashboard, you can see exact geographic locations for all crimes in that type, and hovering over the details (third quadrant) displays further information about each incident.

As you engage with this dashboard, consider the following:

  • Which neighborhoods appear to be the most safe? Which appear to be the least?
  • Do some crimes appear more common in specific neighborhoods?

Exploring the relationship between crime and elevation


While many features and characteristics of a geographical area may contribute to drivers of crime, we intended to explore how elevation specifically may be correlated. Now that you have investigated neighborhoods of interest, consider the following:

  • What can you learn about the elevation in those neighborhoods?
  • What is the relationship between elevation and crime?
  • Do some crime types appear to be more strongly correlated?
